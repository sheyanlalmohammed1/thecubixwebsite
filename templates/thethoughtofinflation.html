The Thought of Inflation

By: Sheyan Lalmohammed on April 9, 2021


Explanation of Theory

Monetary value of goods and services have increased every year, every decade, and every century for as long as the term monetary has been around. This is due to inflation. Inflation, in this case, refers to the decrease in value to all terms of monetary items. This not only refers to currency, but to items that hold monetary value in relation to value of supplemental being as well. A concept that will be dwelled upon later in the study. Now inflation has been attributed too many concepts such as competition, increase in debt, increase in interest rates, over-speculation by the government, and market collapses or recession. This is where an interesting take comes in. Economists agree with the fact that there is a way for political organization to decrease the rate of inflation. This form of policy comes in a different way as there is no actual action but rather a belief or opinion put out by the lawmakers that can combat the problem. This opinion, in simple wording, is the idea that to decrease inflation, lawmakers must convince the people that inflation will not be high. This idealism constitutes a fact that cannot be directly measured by any means, but it does arouse a number of questions. Is the qualification of the theory a basis for analysis into direct growth of inflation? Is inflation attributed to the expectation of inflation? These concepts seem intertwined at first but attribute to a larger expectation of inflation in direct connection. This connection refers to a powerful idea that controls notions of the future of inflation. The people’s thoughts control inflation. To elaborate on this claim, let me simplify the expression in logical terms. I am attempting to argue that the mere thought of inflation being high or low impacts inflation in the future by doing exactly what the people think.


Analysis of Reasoning and Hypothetical Argument

As stated above, there is a lack of direct connection and absence of a scale of measurement as to establish a correlation between thoughts and inflation in immediate terms. This is the proposition of the study though as I believe a direct line can be passed through the actions of large monetary movement to and from the hands of the people. There is an involvement of the government that influences the thoughts of the people as mentioned above. These thoughts then constitute a number of actions that thus incite the rate of inflation to either increase or decrease which is dependent on actions relating to or in line with large monetary value into the lives of consumers. This rate represents a crucial value to lawmakers as they speculate the entirety of the future of the economy in relative terms of their numerical monetary value. This of course is just a claim. A claim which without evidence is rendered useless. Therefore in order to qualify, build upon, and improve this claim, we will look to an abundance of figures and quotations to inductively create this claim from a blank whiteboard and solidify it with the purpose of development of an estimation of inflation rate that draws back to citizens and their thought process.


Expectation of the Study

Theories are more or less incompetent unless able to bet used in a practical sense of endeavor, so to furthermore draw upon the argument of consumer based thinking in relation to inflation rates, a practical study involving current industrial climate could be used. An examination of current political policies as well as the common citizen thought in terms of monetary value in relation with the movement of large monetary value into the lives of American citizens, would prove a practical relation between the two. In order to measure accuracy, the rate of inflation that would come out in terms of the study would be related to that of the World Bank’s “Global Economic Outlook”. This study will be referenced in the evidence portion as it constitutes a majority of the data released about overall economic development, including the effects of inflation.


Place of Evidence

Evidence must clearly show a relation between two fundamentally different concepts. See, to constitute a theory, there must be a clear influx of evidence that will establish a correlation between thought and inflation. Now, because one can only be measured in numerical value while the other is a concept that constantly changes with no evidence as to support why, there must be two completely different concepts of trial in order to constitute a difference between changes in one and the effect on the other. Most would change the numerical value of the inflation rate and trace it back to the thoughts of consumers. This though, would prove to be ineffective since in order to establish a concrete theory as multiple steps of action could lead to a single result from the point of view of the citizens. Rather, I aim to establish two points of thought and delve on the course of logical action that would result in the increase or decrease in the inflation rate. These two points of thought are to be examined as follows. One will establish the thought of a relatively small amount of monetary influx for the year and the other will be the thought of a relatively large amount of monetary influx for the year. In both cases, evidence will be provided in two forms. One form, referred to in future sections as “Evidence for Analysis”, will establish a basis of reasoning based on evidence using inductive methods of investigation. This evidence will be used to create a claim that is solely based on the evidence, a concept referred to as the “Rough Theory”. This claim will be put into practical terms in order to be constituted as a candidate of the established theory by referring to practical application in the second form of evidence, referred to as “Evidence in Practice”. This evidence will draw on already established numbers and actions and the effects they have caused but attempt to create similar conditions with the introduction of the said claim. If able to redeem relatively similar results in terms of inflation, and align with evidence presented in the analysis portion, the Theory would hold a practical application and therefore would constitute that “The Thought of Inflation” does indeed affect inflation.

Evidence Analysis

Figure 2 details an equation known as the Equation of Exchange within the Quantity theory of money. It has four components as labeled above which are “M” or the Money Supply, “V” or the Velocity of Money, “P” or the Average Price Level, and “Q” which in this case will be the real GDP of the economy. This equation is a difficult concept to grasp, but I am going to do my best to break it down, just enough to emphasize the information which it entails. Though looking simple with its four factors, each factor characterizes an undefined relationship of sorts. First, let’s look at money supply, this is simply the amount of money readily available in the hands of people over the course of a defined time period. Then, we have the velocity of money, this time simply measuring the amount of times that the money changes hands from consumers to producers. Next, we have the average price level, this is the amount of money that an average good or service costs on a relative scale to what it is actually worth statistically based on supply and demand. The most common general price level is based on the CPI (Consumer Price Index). Lastly, the Q is going to refer to the real GDP, the real GDP is an inflation-adjusted measure that reflects the value of all goods and services produced by an economy in a given year (expressed in base-year prices). To simplify it a bit more, consider the right side of the equation (the side with P and Q) as being shown of the producers’ output, and the left side of the equation (the side with M and V) shows the consumers’ output. Just for the explanation of it, I’m going to explain why. Starting with the left side, these money supply and velocity of money depend entirely on the consumer’s as the money supply is the cash reserve which the consumers hold and the velocity of money is the rate at which they spend or move that cash supply into the hands of the producers. With the right side, simply put, the goods and services sold are produced by the producers and the overall value of these goods is the real GDP (can also be considered as the total value of all final produced products), and the price level of these goods and services is put into place by the producers as well. With a thorough connection developed between the two, we are going to neutralize the parts of the equation that do not support concrete evidence. The two parts which I am going to take out of account are the velocity of money and the real GDP. The reason being that economists can agree that in a healthy economy, these two values will be, at least in theory, constant. Macroeconomics supports this on a fundamental level, but it is not pertinent to our discussion. What I would like to put attention to is the Money supply and the Price Level. These two, in statistical terms, should directly impact one another in the case of the Equation of Exchange. If the money supply goes up, then so should the Price Level and vice versa. This works in effect with what was stated above in Figure 1.

Figure 3 shows three different charts. The top graph details the inflation rate from the year 2000 to now. Then the bottom graph indicates the relationship between supply and demand, in terms of the calculation of the equilibrium price. Now to establish a connection, we must look at a specific section of the chart detailing the inflation rate in response to real time events. I want to turn to two specific events which can offer opposing data points. One being the Katrina Spike and the other being the deflation in the 2009 recession. Why did the inflation peak after Hurricane Katrina in relation to supply and demand and why did it drop rapidly in the recession of 2009 because of supply and demand? This is the question that the evidence will answer. First, I would like to explain the concept of supply and demand on inflation. To do that, first I would like to bring attention to the second graph. The calculation of the Equilibrium price. I want to establish how the equilibrium price is to be calculated in relation to supply and demand. These concepts are fundamentally tied to the concept of inflation. Supply refers to the total number of completed products that are on the market, and the demand is the relative number of people who have interest in the product at that price point or in general. In the second graph it is detailed as to what happens when the supply is increased as demand remains the same. In the case that this happens, the equilibrium price, the price at which supply meets demand, will fall. This is because there is a surplus of product without an increase in consumers, prompting businesses to drop prices in order to meet returns on investment. Now to further the idea of supply and demand impacting price levels, we will refer to a concept called Demand-Pull Inflation. This is shown in graphs 3 and 4. In these graphs, there are four concepts. The reference of “AD” is aggregate demand, better defined as an economic measurement of the total amount of demand for all finished goods and services produced in an economy. It may also be defined as the total amount of money exchanged for those goods and services at a specific price level and point in time. Then there is the reference to the “SRAS”, called short-run aggregate supply curve. Aggregate supply is defined as the total supply of goods and services that firms in a national economy plan to sell during a specific time period. It is the total amount of goods and services that the firms are willing to sell at a given price level in the economy. There is one reason why this supply is shown in a curve. The curve increases because some nominal input prices are fixed in the short-run and as output rises, more production processes encounter bottlenecks. At low levels of demand, large numbers of production processes do not make full use of their fixed capital equipment. As a result, production can be increased without much diminishing returns. The average price level does not have to rise much in order to justify increased production. In this case, the AS curve is flat. Likewise, when demand is high, there are few production processes that have unemployed fixed outputs. Any increase in demand production causes the prices to increase which results in a steep AS curve. This production output method takes into effect the idea that there is only a certain number of ways to increase production, and once a limit of production capacity is reached, a continued increase in demand will cause a drastic increase in price. The last two concepts are those which have been discussed before, the “PL” or Price Level and the “RGDP” or the Real Gross Domestic Product.

Within the rationale of thinking for consumers, inflation is imminent, and so inflation will nearly never be perceived as being negative, better known as deflation. Rather, periods of deflation will perceive inflation in lower quantities and relatively smaller distribution and range than that of periods of inflation. This is shown through the first two graphs above. We can see that on the left the graph labeled “deflationary period” which shows what the general consumer consensus was on the level of inflation in the U.S. economy during a period of deflation. We can see that the all outstanding opinions add up to 100% relative frequency. Meaning that all bars on both charts add up to 100%, giving us an accurate representation of the total population. To begin with, the chart with the deflationary period has some notable characteristics. Nearly half of the population assumed that inflation would be minimal somewhere in the 0-5 range, which is about average. This means that the people believed that there was just about normal inflation because as a rule of thumb, it is uncommon for any population to ever believe that money will increase in value or prices will drop as many are used to the linear combination of the Consumer Price Index. This can be countered with the phycology during an inflationary period as shown above. The graph clearly shows that while around 18% of the population believes that inflation will stay within the 0-5 range, there is clearly an increase in the relative average of the graph towards the 10-15 range which opens up a new interesting topic as to why there is a greater movement toward an attitude of inflation which creates inflation. The increased average signals that there is a bigger general concern and an increase of risk percentage overall in the economy and creates an opportunity for inflation to truly to take place. The graphs indicate a clear and concise difference between the two situations and therefore explain why there can be supporting evidence for a theory.

The Theory

Let me begin by reiterating my original claim that inflation can be caused by the mere expectation of inflation to go up. Basically stating that only in the positive and somewhat negative direction, inflation is controlled by the people in a state of nonactive control that allows for movement in the direction that comes from a general consensus of such direction. In simple words, people unknowingly make inflation go up and down based on whether they think it will be high or low in that time frame.

The overwhelming amount of practical and analytical evidence proves to be in support of the concept of this stated as inflationary psychology. In this case, the definition of the term meaning the claim of the study. The point was to prove whether the concept is true or not. I can now say with confidence that it is. Let’s run back and explain how I got to this conclusion.

  1. I looked at the MZM Stock and the Consumer Price Index and what exactly the correlation was between the two and what exactly the meaning of the two were in order to identify a relationship. That relationship being that their movements were nearly lined up.
  2. Branching of the idea of the MZM and Inflation, I was brought to the Equation of Exchange in where I was able to decipher that the velocity of money and the money supply were both direct equated to inflation as if either were increased or decreased, there would be changes to inflation. I also established what the price level would represent in the equation as inflation.
  3. Moving off the idea of price level, I figured out how it was calculated in relation to the law of supply and demand. I established the needs of the SRAS and how it could impact the price level and create short term inflation as well as how aggregate demand affected the economy.
  4. Speaking of the Economy, I looked how it reacted in terms of expectations of the CPI/Inflation in times of Inflation and Deflation. I was able to establish that there was a clear connection between a change of mindset between the two which brought up the possibility of a link between thought and inflation.
  5. To set up a change in the mindset between the two mindsets, I used COVID-19 as an example of how some products went up and down in value based on consumer mindset. Some examples that I used were Energy (deflationary), Travel (deflationary), and Grocery (inflationary).
  6. To continue from the point of COVID, we took a real life example of a relatively significant change in inflation and put it against an easy to-see example of change in mindset of inflation. We were finally able to see the entire process in effect and establish its presence.

This isn’t the end of it. There’s no point of deciphering a theory unless it can actually be used to come up with anything useful. In this case, it is the prediction of inflation based on consumer confidence. The proving of the famous economic concept of consumer mindset and inflation presents an opportunity to be used to decipher that mindset and get a roughly accurate reading of the way that inflation will go and at the pace it will change. Will it be major? Minor? Large? Small? Maybe deflation happens? Getting a starting concept with the theory would be its best use for future predictions about inflation. It has a use after all!
